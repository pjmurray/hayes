## Strategy, plain

Tier 1 is **six emails to six people** over ninety minutes on a Thursday morning. That is the entire plan. There is no funnel, no follow-up, no sequence. You send once, in voice, and then you go quiet.

The bet is asymmetric. Five of the six won't reply — that's fine. One quote-tweet from Derek Guy, one Spyplane aside, one Chris Black podcast mention, or one "send me the JA link" from Kinoshita, and HAYES has landed. You only need one to hit.

The reason this works is that these six are **professional tastemakers**. Their job is finding the thing and pointing at it first. A well-voiced cold email with the right URL is not spam to them — it's a tip. You are doing them a small favor by sending it, provided the artifact is good. The site is the artifact. The email's job is to get them to open one link.

**The three rules of Tier 1:**

1. **Personal, not "personalized."** One real line of specific observation from something they wrote in the last 90 days. Not "loved your latest piece" — name the piece and the specific claim inside it that HAYES is in dialogue with.
2. **No ask.** No "would love your thoughts," no "if it resonates, a share would mean the world," no CTA. The URL *is* the ask, and the ask is "look at this."
3. **No follow-up. Ever.** One send, then silence. If they reply, you answer. If they don't, they don't. Following up is the single worst thing you could do — it tells them the voice was performance, not real. Dignified silence is the entire register of HAYES.

## Why personal Gmail, not hello@hayes.press

This is the most load-bearing tactical decision in the whole plan. Five reasons:

1. **Deliverability.** A new domain (hayes.press) with zero outbound sending history is treated hostilely by Gmail and Outlook spam filters — it lands in Promotions or Spam for at least the first ~90 days of consistent sending, which we don't have. Your personal Gmail has years of clean sending reputation. It lands in the Primary tab.
2. **Human signal in the inbox.** `PJ Murray <pj@…>` reads as a person. `HAYES <hello@hayes.press>` reads as a brand. Tier 1 replies to people, not brands. This is the difference between a read and a reply.
3. **Voice.** The email is written in first person — "I have been reading Spyplane since…" A send from a house-domain address pretending to be written by "the house" reads as a corporate mask; the voice collapses.
4. **Reply ergonomics.** You will want to see replies instantly in a familiar inbox and answer in minutes, not hours. Routing through a forwarding rule on hello@hayes.press adds friction exactly when speed matters.
5. **Parasocial calibration.** Spyplane, Derek Guy, Chris Black — their inboxes are full of brand pitches they ignore and personal notes from readers they occasionally answer. Your goal is category-two.

**The one caveat:** set your Gmail `From` display name to `PJ Murray` (not `PJ @ HAYES` or `PJ Murray (HAYES)`). The mention of HAYES belongs in the subject line and the body, not the sender field. Let them click before they know.

**Tier 2 and Tier 3 also go from personal Gmail.** hello@hayes.press is only for inbound correspondence via the site form and for people who already know HAYES.

## The five questions

Answer these in writing before Wed night. Each one materially changes the plan.

### 1. Do you have any warm connections — even weak ones — to any of the six?

A single "my old coworker used to work with Kurt" is worth ten cold sends. "Warm" doesn't mean close; it means a real person can pass a three-line note that starts "PJ is a friend, and he made this." If anyone lights up, we route that target through the intro, not the cold send, and the probability of reply triples.

### 2. Should we mail a printed card with a handwritten note to each Tier 1 in addition to the email?

We have 100 Moo cards. Mailing six cards with a handwritten sentence on the back — so they arrive Mon/Tue of the following week, after the Thursday email has sat in their head — doubles the surface area of the outreach for about $10. A physical card on the desk a week later is exactly the kind of second touch an editorial brand gets away with (and a commercial brand can't).

**Recommendation:** yes. Pre-address the six envelopes Tue Apr 21, post Fri Apr 24 (a day after the email). Each note is one sentence, in ink: *"— if this reached you, thank you for reading. PJ."* That's it.

But it requires addresses, which is a different hunt. Confirm per target.

### 3. Which landing page for each target — is my routing right?

My current proposal:
- Jonah + Erin → `/` (homepage)
- Derek Guy → `/antecedents`
- Chris Black → `/antecedents`
- Kurt Soller → `/` (homepage)
- Kyle Chayka → `/` (homepage)
- Kinoshita → `/ja/antecedents`

Worth confirming because the first click frames everything — and Derek/Chris landing on Antecedents vs homepage is a different brand impression entirely.

### 4. If one of them replies with "how do I buy" or "is this real" — what's the actual answer?

My drafts above assume "there's no inventory, Volume 01 is a periodical, waitlist is the register." Is that the honest truth, or is there a quiet plan to ship garments if Vol 01 lands? If a single Tier 1 generates 100 people asking to buy, what do they actually hit?

This is the one business question that *can't* be answered in voice. Decide it now — even if the answer is "no inventory, ever, it's parody" — so the reply playbook is truthful.

### 5. What's your Gmail sending reputation like, and will you risk the primary address?

If your personal Gmail is also your GQ work address or receives hundreds of transactional emails a day, Google sometimes throttles new thread velocity from the same account. Six sends in 90 minutes to people who have never received mail from you is a minor flag — not a blocker, but worth knowing. If you'd rather use a clean secondary personal Gmail (one you've owned for years but use less), that's equally fine.

Also: do you want replies going to the same inbox you're reading at 11pm on a Tuesday, or do you want them routed somewhere calmer? The answer affects which account you send from.
